# Who Owns Experience?

## On the Transfer of Human Knowledge into Neural Models

**Author:** Markus Schatzl (technology.culture@proton.me)

**Status:** Working paper, Janaury 2026

---

## Abstract

AI systems extract not just data but experience patterns—decision logics, heuristics, tacit knowledge. This extraction happens incidentally, through the mere use of digital tools. The question of who owns this experience—and under what conditions it may be transferred—remains unanswered.

This paper analyzes the problem in three contexts: employees whose experiential knowledge is preserved in enterprise systems; researchers whose unpublished ideas flow into training corpora; children whose learning processes are captured by educational platforms. In all three cases, the person loses control over something constitutive—not over a product, but over the capability that enables products.

The central thesis holds: Experience is neither private property nor common good. It is conditionally shareable—under conditions of consent and reciprocity. For this conditional shareability, however, no legal framework exists. The paper sketches research questions that would need to be addressed on the path to such a framework.

The paper proceeds from the assumption that individual control over experience is a good worth protecting. This assumption itself is debatable—and part of the questions this paper seeks to raise.

---

## 1. Starting Point

When an engineer uses an AI system through a corporate account over months—whether Microsoft Copilot, ChatGPT Enterprise, or an industry-specific system—far more emerges than a collection of work products. A digital profile of their decision patterns emerges: how they analyze problems, which factors they prioritize, which solution paths they prefer. Decades of industry knowledge, process understanding, and error wisdom become contextualized and preserved in the system.

When a researcher thinks through an immature hypothesis with an LLM, plays through a methodological innovation, or explores a new theoretical approach, this exploratory thinking flows into platforms whose terms of service provide for use in model training—without timestamp, without attribution, without control.

When a child uses adaptive learning systems, their learning trajectories, error types, and thought processes are captured—cognitive patterns that form in the process of learning. This data flows into platforms whose implications can be grasped neither by the child nor meaningfully by the parents.

What emerges in all three cases is a functional representation of human experience—a digital counterpart to the person themselves. The question of who owns this counterpart is unanswered.

---

## 2. What Is Extracted: Data vs. Experience

A distinction is fundamental: Data is discrete and attributable. A name, a birth date, an IP address—these are data. They can be collected, stored, deleted. The GDPR regulates them.

Experience is something else. It is relational: it emerges in interaction with other people, with materials, with situations. Without the contribution of others, experience is not possible. At the same time, it depends substantially—if not almost entirely—on individual perception and the capacity to connect impressions, which is clearly individual.

Experience is thus neither purely individually nor purely socially constituted. It emerges in the in-between space—and precisely for this reason it is not captured by existing categories.

## 3. Three Contexts

### 3.1 Employee

In labor law, it is established that artifacts—work products, inventions, documents—transfer to the employer. The competence to produce these artifacts remains with the employee. This boundary was functional as long as both were separable.

AI-supported work environments blur the boundary. The interaction itself becomes the artifact. Prompts, iterations, feedback—everything is stored. The before—the competence that precedes the work product—becomes visible and preservable.

What should leave with the employee stays in the system. After termination of employment, the experience profile remains with the employer. The departed employee has effectively no way to prevent the continued use of their knowledge.

A second layer compounds this: The operators of AI platforms reserve in their terms of service the right to use user inputs for further development of their models. Thus the specialized knowledge of individual experts flows not only into the enterprise system but potentially also into the training foundation of global AI models—without knowledge or consent of the knowledge bearers.

### 3.2 Researcher

In science, intellectual achievement is the currency. Reputation, career, funding, appointments—everything depends on ideas, methods, and findings being attributed to a person. The citation system is at its core an attribution system: whoever publishes first gets the idea attributed to them.

AI interaction undermines this system. Unpublished hypotheses, methodological considerations, exploratory thoughts flow into platforms—without timestamp, without citation, without peer review. The attributability of intellectual achievement erodes before the achievement can even be published.

Unlike the work context, there is no existing boundary being shifted here. The citation system protects publications—but there is no protective framework for what precedes publication. And that is precisely where the most valuable lies: the idea in statu nascendi.

### 3.3 Child

Adaptive learning systems capture learning trajectories, error types, thought processes—cognitive patterns that form in the process of learning. This data flows into platforms whose terms of use can be grasped neither by the child nor meaningfully by the parents.

Here there exists neither a labor law nor a scientific framework. There is no boundary being shifted—there simply is no boundary. Only extraction.

The question of consent arises here with particular sharpness: Can parents consent on behalf to something whose long-term consequences no one can assess? And even if they could—do they do so informedly when they accept the cookie banner of an educational platform?

---

## 4. The Common Denominator

In all three contexts, the person loses control over something constitutive—not over a product they make, but over the capability that enables products.

The cognitive signature is externalized.

**For the employee:** The experiential knowledge that grounds their position in the labor market is preserved and remains available after they have left.

**For the researcher:** The intellectual achievement that grounds their reputation flows away before they can publish it.

**For the child:** The learning processes that constitute their development are captured before they are capable of disposing over them.

The structure is the same in all three cases: Experience as substrate is transferred—without consent, without compensation, without recoverability.

---

## 5. Why Existing Categories Don't Apply

**Private property:** Experience is not fully individually produced. It emerges in interaction with others—with colleagues, teachers, materials, situations. The claim that experience is pure private property overlooks the social share of its emergence.

**Common good:** But this does not mean experience is available for commercial extraction. That is the short circuit the platform economy makes: what is not explicitly protected is free. But "not fully private" does not mean "available to anyone who expresses interest."

**Personal data (GDPR):** The GDPR protects identifiable data. Whether accumulated experience profiles from interaction histories are to be classified as personal data is unclear. The deletion of individual knowledge contributions from a trained model is technically barely feasible.

**Works (Copyright):** Copyright protects creations—concrete works with recognizable form. It does not protect ways of thinking, heuristics, or decision patterns. What is extracted, however, is precisely that.

**Work products (Labor law):** Labor law regulates artifacts. It does not regulate what precedes them—the competence, the experiential knowledge, the cognitive structure that enables artifacts. Precisely this boundary is blurred by AI.

---

## 6. Thesis: Conditional Shareability

Experience is neither private property nor common good. It is conditionally shareable—under conditions of consent and reciprocity.

The analogy to open source is instructive: Free software is not ownerless. It is consciously released under conditions—GPL, MIT, Creative Commons. The author retains rights but grants use under defined rules. This works because there is a legal framework (copyright) on which licenses can build.

For experiential knowledge, this framework is missing. There is no experience law on which an experience license could build. Therefore release is not treated as a conscious act but as default.

Voluntary sharing—in the sense of open source, public domain, charitable contributions—would be conceivable under these conditions. Whether it can count as an act of sovereignty depends on whether a genuine choice exists. Without choice, the question arises whether we should speak of sharing or extraction.

## 7. Research Questions

The preceding analysis opens questions that cannot be answered in purely technical or purely legal terms:

1. What legal status would accrue to experiential knowledge that is neither fully individually nor fully socially constituted? Are there precedents in other legal domains—for instance in dealing with traditional knowledge of indigenous communities?

2. What conditions would need to be fulfilled for the transfer of experience into neural models to count as consensual? Is a cookie banner enough? Is a terms of service clause enough? Or are more specific forms of disclosure and consent required?

3. How could a right of revocation be conceived that accounts for the accumulation of experience data over time? The GDPR knows a right to deletion—but what does deletion mean for a trained model?

4. What institutional structures would be needed to make conditional shareability enforceable? Are trustees needed, registries, new forms of collective rights administration?

5. How does the argument developed here relate to existing concepts such as the right to informational self-determination or the right to one's own image? Is there a right to one's own thought pattern?

## 8. What Is at Stake

The dynamic described is not static. It accelerates with each model generation—larger context windows, finer pattern recognition, longer storage of interaction histories. What appears today as a marginal problem will be structurally defining tomorrow.

### 8.1 Autonomy as Disposal over One's Own

Autonomy presupposes that there is something that can be disposed over. Something that is mine—not in the sense of possession, but in the sense of: it belongs to me, it constitutes me, it distinguishes me from others.

If this own—the way I think, decide, approach problems—is externalized and replicable, the question arises: What remains of autonomy when its core is already available elsewhere?

### 8.2 Devaluation Through Replication

The problem is not only extraction but replication. As long as experiential knowledge remains in a person's head, it is scarce—and this scarcity grounds value: salary, position, reputation, bargaining power.

When the same knowledge becomes available in a model, scarcity disappears. The person whose experience was extracted now competes with a copy of themselves—a copy that doesn't sleep, doesn't negotiate, doesn't quit.

This is not science fiction. It is the explicit goal of many AI implementation projects: to preserve the knowledge of experienced employees before they retire. The employees themselves articulate the consequence precisely: "Once you've trained the system, you won't need us anymore."

### 8.3 Implications for the Commonwealth

In sum, a shift occurs: experiential knowledge that was previously distributed among many individuals accumulates with few institutions—the operators of platforms, the holders of models.

This concentration changes power relations: Whoever aggregates the experience of many can potentially act without them. The question is what role the many then still play—as bearers, as sources, or as something in between.

This is not dystopian speculation but the extrapolation of an already visible dynamic. The question is whether and how it can be politically shaped—and whether such shaping is even considered necessary.

---

## 9. Scope

The considerations developed here focus on AI-supported environments. They are, however, structurally applicable to other forms of digital interaction.

The extraction of experience patterns has not happened only since the emergence of large language models. Every captcha service that uses human classification performance for model training; every analytics platform that aggregates behavioral patterns; every educational platform that stores learning trajectories—they all operate according to the same structure: experience is extracted without the affected person having control.

The difference from LLM interactions is gradual, not categorical. But the dialogic nature of LLM interaction makes the extraction visible—and thus politicizable.

---

## 10. Conclusion

The question "Who owns experience?" is not a technical question. It concerns the relationship between individual, collective, and the institutions that accumulate knowledge.

The answer will shape the relationship between digital infrastructures and individual autonomy—in whatever direction.

This paper understands itself as a discussion basis. It formulates a problem that is structurally unaddressed and sketches directions in which answers might lie. The actual work—legal, philosophical, political—lies ahead.
